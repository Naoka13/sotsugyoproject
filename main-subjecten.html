Religion/History

About Shinto(Shintoism)

The origin of Shinto goes back to ancient Japan. Based on rice farming that was introduced from the end of the Jomon period to the Yayoi period, it is said that the "nature worship" that gods dwell in the natural world such as the sea, mountains, and trees arose in the Japanese archipelago. Before long, people began to believe that gods resided not only in the natural world but also in business, learning, homes, rice, and everything else, and the gods in Shinto came to be called 'eight million gods.' It has played an important role in all aspects of life for ancient people who lived in harmony with nature. In Shinto, the god of the sun, Amaterasu Omikami, is considered the supreme deity, and its descendant is said to be the emperor.

About Buddhism

Buddhism was founded by Gautama Siddhartha in India around the 4th to 6th century BC. It is said that it crossed over from India to China, and then came to Japan from China around the 6th century. In addition, Buddhism in Japan was popularized by Prince Shotoku, and he built many temples throughout Japan, including Horyuji and Shitennoji, and spread the thought of Buddhism.

Tendai sect

The Tendai sect is a sect of Buddhism founded by Tendai Daishi Chigi (Monks from the period of the Northern and Southern Dynasties in China to the Sui Dynasty) in Tiantai County, Zhejiang Province, China during the Sui dynasty. The Tendai sect preached the teaching of the Lotus Ichijo, which states that all people can become Buddhas. It is also called the mother mountain of Buddhism because the founders of each sect such as Honen (A Japanese monk from the late Heian period to the early Kamakura period.) and Shinran (Japanese Buddhists in the early to middle Kamakura period) learned the Tendai sect.

Shingon sect

Shingon Buddhism is a sect of Mahayana Buddhism founded by Kukai (Monks in the early Heian period) in the early 9th century (Heian period) and is one of Japanese Buddhism. It is based on the esoteric Buddhism that Kukai learned from Eka at Seiryu-ji Temple after traveling to Changan. He spread the teaching that anyone can become a Buddha immediately by acting in the same way as a Buddha called "Sokushin Jobutsu" and keeping a pure heart.

Jodo sect

Jodo sect is one of the Japanese Buddhism founded by Honen in 1175. The Jodo sect preached a doctrine called ``Kanmuryojukyosho,'' in which one simply chants the Nembutsu without doing any other practice. In addition, according to the Jodo sect, chanting Namu Amidabutsu purifies the mind and body, and after death, one can be reborn in the Pure Land and become a Buddha.

Jodo Shinsyu

Jodo Shinshu is one of the Pure Land sects and is also known as Kamakura Buddhism. It was opened by Shinran, a disciple of Honen, the founder of the Jodo sect. Pure Land Buddhism is a teaching of absolute other power that advocates the salvation of all people by Amida Buddha (In Buddhism of the Pure Land sect, Amida belief is preached that one can be reborn in the Pure Land through the shomyo nenbutsu called 'Namu Amida Butsu'.) and is characterized by the idea that one can attain Buddhahood immediately if one is born with faith. With over 12 million followers nationwide, Jodo Shinshu is the largest Buddhist force in Japan.

Buddhism(from the Meiji era)

In 1872, the Jinshin Family Register (The first nationwide family register by the Meiji government.) changed the status of monks into a profession. One of the characteristics is that modern Buddhist studies were developed by learned monks who studied abroad, and Buddhist enlightenment activities and lay Buddhist movements began.

The reasons why Japan factored in Buddhism

The person who spread Buddhism is said to be Prince Shotoku (Imperial Family and Politicians of the Asuka Period). However, when Buddhism was first introduced, there was a conflict between the Soga clan (supporters) and the Mononobe clan (opposition) over whether to accept Buddhism. Therefore, Prince Shotoku cooperated with the Soga clan to overthrow the Mononobe clan and spread Buddhism. It is said that he was aiming for a centralized state centered on the emperor by using Buddhist thought.

More

However, there is Shinto at the base of Japan. In Shinto, the descendants of Amaterasu Omikami are regarded as emperors, which means that if the emperor believes in Buddhism, the ancient Japanese gods will come under Buddhism. Therefore, to make Shinto and Buddhism compatible, ``Shinbutsu Shugo'' was created, which equates Japanese gods with Buddhism.

Events

Festival【 Shinto 】

The origin of the festival is described in the Kojiki (Records of Ancient Matters) (712) in the myth of " Amaterasu’s hiding away in the cave". Amaterasu Oomikami, the sun god who illuminates the world, and Susanoo no Mikoto, the younger brother of the god of the sea. Susanoo has a bad temper and always causes trouble for those around him. Because Amaterasu hid in the cave, the world became dark and many disasters occurred. It is said that the origin of the festival is that the eight million gods began to make a fuss in front of the rock to get Amaterasu out of the rock.

More

Summer festivals also have their own origins. In Japan, festivals have been held every four seasons since ancient times, and the meaning and purpose of the festivals differ depending on the season. Many of the summer festivals are said to have originated from driving away pest typhoons that interfere with good harvests. In addition, summer is the season when epidemics tend to spread, so there are summer festivals aimed at driving away epidemics.
For example, the “Gion Festival'' was held at Yasaka Shrine in Kyoto is said to have its origins in 869 in the Heian period, when a goryoe(ceremony to appease evil gods and the spirits) was held to contain the epidemic that spread in Kyoto. It is a summer festival that boasts a large scale.

New Year【 Shinto 】

New Year is an event where the whole family welcomes Toshigami, who comes to bring happiness for the year. Toshigami is said to be a god who brings good health, prosperity to descendants and a bountiful harvest. It is said that kadomatsu (It is the New Year’s decorative pine branches), shimekazari (Decorating shrines and gates with shimenawa ropes for the New Year), and kagami-mochi (It is two-round piled up rice cakes) are preparations to welcome the god of the new year.

More

New Year is the oldest event in Japan, and there are still many mysteries about its origin.
According to one theory, it is said to have already existed in the middle of the 6th century, and it is thought that the belief in the god of agriculture and the ancestors combined to create the god who protects the house. It is said that the origin of New Year's Day is that the old Japanese people welcomed the god of the new year at the beginning of the year and entertained them to bring happiness and prosperity to their homes.
There are also things related to the New Year such as osechi (It is the traditional meal only prepared for New Year). It is said that osechi ryori was prepared as a form of hospitality for the New Year's deity. It has been said that by offering food to the gods and eating them at the same time, you will receive the blessings of the gods. In addition, each osechi dish has auspiciousness and various meanings.

Hatsumode【 Shinto 】

Hatsumode is defined as "the first visit to a shrine or temple since the beginning of the new year." And one of the events that is said to be the origin of Hatsumode is "Toshikamori". Toshigomori means "the head of a village or family shuts himself up at a shrine or temple where the local deity is enshrined from the night of New Year's Eve to the morning of New Year's Day without sleeping." And the custom of praying for a good harvest and safety in the new year through the night has been handed down to this day.

More

Toshigomori eventually came to be divided into two events: visiting shrines and temples on the night of New Year's Eve and the morning of New Year's Day. A visit on New Year's Eve is called "New Year's Day", and a visit on New Year's Day is called "New Year's Day". On New Year's Day, it was customary to visit the shrines and temples that enshrine the tutelary deity of the area where you live, or the shrines and temples that are in the lucky direction of the year when viewed from your home. This was later called "Ehomiri". It originated in the Kansai region and is said to be related to Ehomaki sushi roll (It is thick sushi roll which is believed to bring good fortune if eaten while facing the year’s “Eho”(good luck direction), which is now familiar all over Japan. In addition, depending on the region, there is still a custom of visiting shrines and temples twice on New Year's Eve and New Year's Day, and this is called "Ninen-mairi". "New Year's Day Visit" and "New Year's Day Visit" were performed, but the custom remained only on New Year's Day, and it became common to ``freely visit famous shrines and temples'' for Eho. did.

Setsubun【 Shinto/Buddhism 】

Setsubun refers to the day before the beginning of each season (start of spring, start of summer, start of autumn, start of winter), and also means "dividing the seasons". After the Edo period, it often refers to the day before Risshun (around February 4th every year). It is generally said to ward off evil spirits and wish for good health.

More

The history of Setsubun dates to the Heian period, when the imperial court event "Tsuina (Oni Yarai)" was held. In the Heian period, tsuina was held on New Year's Eve in December, and the role of demons called hosouji went around the court with officials who played the role of subordinates to ward off evil. The demon had four golden eyes, wore a vermilion costume, and wielded a shield and a spear. At first, it was a good deity who exorcised evil spirits with its appearance, but around the 9th century, it came to be regarded as an evil spirit and was chased with a bow and arrow. Oni symbolized plague, and people tried to contain the epidemic by warding off demon-shaped plagues with bows and arrows. After that, Setsubun spread to the common people as Setsubun festivals were held at temples and shrines where people used to gather.

Easter【 Christianity 】

Easter means "resurrection". Specifically, it is believed that Jesus Christ, the founder of Christianity, was crucified and died, but three days later, Christ was resurrected. Celebrating the resurrection is "Easter" and it is considered as an important event as "Christmas" in Christianity.

More

It is said there is an image that Easter is an event that takes place in spring. There is also a reason for this. Easter is said to be derived from the name of the goddess of spring in ancient Germanic mythology, Eoster. This is because the resurrection of Jesus Christ and the image of the goddess symbolizing spring are common, and Easter also includes the meaning of celebrating the arrival of spring. Also, there is a definition that Easter is ``the first Sunday counting from the first full moon after the vernal equinox''. By the way, "Easter Island", which is famous for its moai statues, is said to be associated with being discovered on Easter Day (April 5) in 1722. On the other hand, "rabbit" is said to be a prolific animal (giving birth to many children) and is a symbol of the prosperity and fertility of offspring.

Tanabata【 Buddhism 】

Bon【 Shinto/Buddhism 】

Obon is officially called "Urabon-e", which is said to be derived from the Buddhist sutra "Urabon-kyo". It refers to the period during which the deceased and ancestors return from the world called the afterlife (Pure Land) to this world.

More

The urabon in the urabon sutra is derived from the Sanskrit word urabanna (hanging upside down), which is said to represent a legend about one of Buddha's disciples, Mokuren (An ancient Indian monk and one of the ten great disciples of Shakyamuni Buddha.)
The legend is that when Mokuren learned that his late mother was being hanged upside down, he asked Buddha how to save her mother. And it is said that the current customs of Obon started from the Buddha's teaching to "hold a memorial service on July 15 (in the lunar calendar)." In addition, there are “Mukaebi”, “Okuribi”, and “Bon Odori” in Obon. “Mukaebi” is a fire that is lit so that the spirits of ancestors can return to this world without hesitation. On the other hand, "Okuribi" is a fire to send off the ancestors who spent the Obon festival together to the other world. And "Bon Odori" is a dance that people dance to liven up the last night when their ancestors return to the other world.

Shichi-go-san【 Shinto/Buddhism 】

It is the festival that is for boys aged three and five, and girls aged three and seven. The origin of Shichi-go-san dates to the Heian period. At that time, medical care was not as advanced as it is today, and the mortality rate for children was high. For this reason, Shichi-go-san is said to have originated from the court and court nobles thanking the gods for their children's growth at the ages of 3, 5 and 7.

More

At Shichi-go-san, different ceremonies were held for each age group: 3-year-olds "Kamioki-no-gi", 5-year-olds "Hakamagi-no-gi", and 7-year-olds "Obitoki-no-gi". rice field. First, what is the "Kamioki-no-gi" In the Heian period, it was customary to raise a child with a shaved head until the age of three, and it was believed that keeping the head clean would prevent illness and grow healthy hair. Therefore, this ritual was performed when starting to grow hair from the age of three. The Hakamagi-no-gi ceremony, which is held at the age of 5, was originally a ceremony for both men and women, but changed to a ceremony for boys only during the Edo period. The ritual starts with standing on a go board, which means to take over the world, facing the lucky direction, and wearing a hakama from the left foot, which is said to be auspicious. It is also said that he wore a crown and worshiped the gods of the four directions, with the hope of defeating the enemies on all four sides. And finally, the Obi-de-no-gi, which is held at the age of 7, is a ceremony to celebrate that a child who was wearing a kimono with a string sewn on will wear an obi and wear a kimono like an adult. All of them were held at the milestone of the child's growth, and there was a meaning to wish for the safety of the future.

Christmas【 Christianity 】

Christmas is "the day to celebrate the birth of Jesus Christ", that is, the day to celebrate that Christ was born into this world, not the birthday of Christ. Generally, the time of Christmas is from sunset on December 24th to sunset on December 25th (sunset is the boundary of the day in the Jewish calendar and the church calendar). However, it is not yet known exactly when and how Christmas began.

More

There is one prevailing theory as to why Christmas is on December 25th. December 25th is associated with the Sun God Mithras' Festival of Lights. The festival of light was held on the winter solstice, the shortest day of the year, and celebrated the resurrection of the sun. And based on this, the Roman emperor compared Christ to "sun and light" to spread Christianity for political reasons and adopted Mithra's "Festival of Lights" as the birthday of Christ. It is said to have originated from the 25th of the month.
Also, there are various items for Christmas, but here I will introduce a little about "Christmas tree". The Christmas tree is also known as the "tree of the knowledge of good and evil". This is believed in Christianity, and there was a tree bearing fruit that God forbade the first human beings "Adam" and "Eve" to eat. They were told that they could eat the fruit of the other trees in the Garden of Eden where they lived, but not the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. The reason is that by eating this, humans gain knowledge and power and have the same power as God. However, they were seduced by a snake that was there and ate the fruit of the tree and were banished from the Garden of Eden by God. From such a story, it is said that the red decorations on the current Christmas tree represent the forbidden fruit that Adam and Eve ate.

Japan’s staple food is rice, and there is a close relationship between Japan, rice, and religion. In Shinto, it's said that a god who is named Niniginomikoto get ear of rice from Amaterasu Omikami who is his grandfather when he descended on Japan, and he gave it to the people. As a result, rice has been offered as sacred food in various festivals.

More

Shinto has existed in Japan since that time and Amaterasu Omikami is a sun god, and Niniginomikoto is a fertility god and they were believed. Moreover, it’s said that their descendant is a Japanese emperor. The number of Japanese people who know this is low in modern days and depending on our research, the ratio of it was about 20%.

Japanese tea【 Buddhism 】

Japanese tea culture started in the early Heian era (794-1185). In China, there are some denominations of Buddhism, and it had drunk tea that includes caffeine as a method to suppress sleep in the Zen sect. The beginning of the Japanese tea culture is Eichu who is a priest presented it to Emperor Saga. After so many years apart, that tea spread to ordinary people.

More

Emperor Saga so liked that tea that he issued an Imperial ordinance about growing the teas. The tea at that time isn’t a recreational drink but it was drunk for health and luxury drinks. As a result, that tea didn’t popular with ordinary people and went out of it.
However, Eisai, the founder of the Rinzai sect, who was training in China in 1191 was interested in the effect of that tea and took it away to Japan. By this, tea culture was revived in Japan. The Rinzai sect is one of Buddhism’s denominations. The tea that was taken away to Japan by Eisai is a kind of tea called “Hiki cha”, and this is like matcha. At first, the Hiki cha spread in Zen temples and also spread to samurai gradually. For samurai whose had trained strictly every day, the Zen sect was a religion that is easy to accept in that trained the mind, and that tea also spread immediately.
In the Edo era, the Obaku sect of Zen sect gained power. Baisao who is the priest of Obaku sect started to sell teas in Kyoto in 1734. By doing this, Japanese tea culture not only samurai and priests but also spread to ordinary people.
I described “Japanese tea culture started in the early Heian era”, but the tea culture at that time was gone out once, so it’s said the origin of contemporary of Japanese tea culture is the tea that was spread by Eisai.

Buddhist cuisine【 Buddhism 】

There are some Buddhist cuisines that are called “Shojin cuisine” in Japan. Buddhist cuisine is a cuisine don’t make with meat and fish. It’s said to come with Buddhism It’s said to come with Buddhism from China and was made in order to not take a life and, renounce worldly desire.

“Shojin” is a Buddhist terminology and means “mental training by having a plain and vegetarian diet”.。
The ingredients for Buddhist cuisine are allowed to use vegetables, cereals, seaweed, beans, nuts, and fruits. Also, there are 5 cooking methods raw, boil, grill, frying, and steaming. The Buddhist cuisine of Japan includes simmered, sesame tofu, tempura, and kenchin-jiru soup.

Sumo【 Shinto 】

Sumo is a national sport of Japan. According to the Nihon Shoki, which is Japan’s oldest written account, it’s said that sumo originated when Nomino Sukune and Taima No Kehaya competed in strength in front of Emperor Suinin. The winner was Nomino Sukune at that time, and he has been enshrined in over 20 shrines as an ancestor of sumo since then.
Sumo was performed as an annual religious ceremony that pray for the harvest of the crops in the Nara era(710-794). When the Heian era(794-1185), it evolved as a palace event. Now, it has been held “Shinji sumo” which is wrestled at the shrine for dedicate to the god every year.

Shrine and Temple【 Shinto/Buddhism 】

The shrine is a religious facility for Shinto, and the temple is it for Buddhism. The main difference between them is the shrine is for the god lives, and there is a torii, and on the other hand, the temple is for the priest lives, and there is a Buddha statue. Many Japanese sometimes visit the shrine for the festival and the first visit of the year to the shrine and also visit the temple for funerals and graves.

More

There are Ise Jingu, Itsukushima Shrine, Izumo Taisha, and so on as the famous shrine. Ise Jingu is said to the top grade shrine because Amaterasu Oomikami who is an ancestor of the Japanese emperor enshrined in it. Ichikishimahime, Tagorihime and Tagitsuhime whose are goddesses are enshrined in Itukushima Shrine. They are called “Munakata Three Goddess”, and also are enshrined in Munakata Taisha. They are famous for gods of entertainment, Traffic/Sailing safety, and luck with money. Ookuninushi is enshrined in Izumo Taisha. He is famous for a god of marriage, and also is said to have built a nation on the ground.
There are Kiyomizu temple, Kinkaku-ji, Toudai-ji, and so on as the famous temple. The temple doesn’t enshrine the god. It was originally the place to learn the teachings of Buddhism, the propagation, and the intercultural exchange, but now it’s often visited for funerals, Houji(Buddhist memorial service, held on the anniversary of a person’s death), and sightseeing. Kiyomizu temple is famous for not only sightseeing spot, but also “Kanji of the year”. This is a Kanji that is symbolized this year. Kinkaku-ji is an alias name, and “Rokuon-ji” is the real name. This is a World Cultural Heritage as one of Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto. Toudai-ji has the famous Great Buddha of Nara which is nearly 15 meters. Also, it’s famous for exists many deer around here, but it’s believed the deer is the messenger of God in Shinto, and they have been protected since Kasuga Taisha was built. Toudai-ji has nothing to do with the deer.

The deer of Nara【 Shinto 】

There is a lot of wild deer in Nara Prefecture, and they are designated as a national natural monument. Because it’s believed the deer is the messenger of God in Shinto. By “Koshaki” in Kasugataisha Shrine(the document that is written about history of Kasugataisha Shrine), it’s written about the God had descended to earth by riding the deer. The deer have been protected as the messenger of God since then.

Princess Mononoke【 Shinto 】

“Princess Mononoke” is one of the Studio Ghibli movie that is made by Hayao Miyazaki in 1997. Above “the Spirited Away” was also made by him. The Princess Mononoke is a story about “Ashitaka”, who is cursed his one arm by “Tatarigami”, take a journey to “the forest of Shishigami” to find the way for break a curse. Tatarigami is like a god have curse and hatred for human who break nature. Shishigami is a god that preside over life and death. The nature worship of Shinto is to worship mystic power and existence that reside in nature and natural phenomenon. In Shinto, it’s believed spirits and gods reside in trees, rocks, mountains, etc., natural disasters that tsunami, earthquake, etc., is a kind of curse of gods.
Princess Mononoke is so to speak, a story about the conflict between humans and nature. It's developed “Isibiya” (the ancient weapon like a gun) caused by the development of steelmaking technology, and humans use it for killing humans and animals. Ishibiya was a weapon that actually existed in the Muromachi era(1336-1573), so this movie is set in this era. And mankind who try to expand their territory take the mountain from animals by cutting down trees and cultivating a mountain, and the big boar that is a guardian of the mountain is shot by them, and the boar becomes a tatarigami. In addition, they try to kill Shishigami that presides over life and death to get immortality. Shishigami is a motif of deer. In Shinto, the deer is thought messenger of god, so I think shishigami is in the shape of a deer.
Miyazaki Hayao often includes religious view of Shinto to his works. I guess that he aimed to make audience think about how to face nature through the natural worship of Shinto.
